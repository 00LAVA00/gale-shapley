Introduction

Gale-Shapley algorithm, introduced by economists David Gale and Lloyd Shapley, is a mathematical solution to the stable marriage problem. Originally devised to optimize pairing in a scenario where individuals have preferences over one another, this algorithm ensures stable and satisfactory matches by employing a systematic approach of proposals and rejections. The algorithm finds its applications beyond romantic pairings, making it a fundamental tool in various matching scenarios. Considering a group of 'n' proposers (formerly known as "men") and recipients (formerly known as "women"), each with their individual preferences, our objective is to establish an assignment that satisfies the following conditions:

  • Each proposer is paired with exactly one recipient.
  • No pair of individuals, not already in a partnership, would choose to leave their current partners for each other.

This concept is commonly referred to as a "stable matching," and its applications span various fields such as economics, networks, medical school assignments, and, of course, romantic pairings.

The Gale-Shapley Algorithm offers a pragmatic solution to this problem. It proceeds by processing proposers arbitrarily and navigating through each proposer's list of preferences for recipients. During each iteration, if a recipient is unengaged, the (proposer, recipient) pair forms a temporary "engagement." If the recipient is already engaged, it will only switch partners if it prefers the current proposer more. This process continues until all 'n' proposers and recipients are engaged with one another.

The algorithm is proven to yield a unique and optimal solution, operating with quadratic time complexity.
